AI and the future of design systems

The source of truth is moving from design to code

In an AI-native workflow, running code becomes the canonical artifact. Design files still play an important role, but more as a space to align on intent and gather feedback, rather than the thing everyone builds against.

For years, the default assumption was that the design file was the source of truth. Figma held the canonical screens, engineering implemented them, and if the build didn't match the file, the build was considered wrong. The artifact led, and code followed. Yet the file was only ever treated as the source of truth, and never actually was.

In an AI-native workflow, that dynamic changes. Intent can now move almost directly into working software: agents generate and maintain the code, and the running system is what users actually experience. The design file is no longer where the truth lives. It becomes a proposal for the truth, useful right up until the code exists.

The shift is not that design stops mattering. It is that the drawn artifact stops being the contract and becomes the conversation. The designer's role remains essential: guiding decisions, advocating for the user, and ensuring the product reflects the right intent. The function of design does not change at its core; only the medium does.

Where tokens, the design system & component specs live

source of truth a view we discuss, not canonical

The source of truth moves to code. AI agents build from a locked design-system contract, then render a visual layer we can discuss.

And what happens to the design system?

In the old model, the design system lived in Figma as a library of styles and component specs that engineers re-created by hand. In the new model, it becomes a locked contract in code: tokens, component specs, and rules that are typed, linted, and tested.

It also explains why translating Figma straight to code with AI keeps misfiring. Pixels under-specify intent, so any picture-to-code path is lossy and the model fills the gaps by guessing, no matter how capable it gets. A contract removes the ambiguity: the agent builds from defined rules instead of inferring them.

AI agents use that contract to generate the running code and render the visual layer, so the picture and the product come from the same source. A design system that exists only in Figma becomes a suggestion the generator can ignore.

Why render it into Figma at all?

If the app is built from the same contract, why not just review the running code? Because Figma gives us something the app does not: a frozen, annotatable, side-by-side surface built for discussion.

Over time, that surface may not be Figma at all. Designers might choose a quick HTML render, Figma, or another visual tool depending on the conversation they need to have.

Why the visual layer still matters

None of this makes the visual layer irrelevant. It simply changes its role. Instead of being drawn by hand as the source of truth, it becomes a way to review, compare, and discuss what the system is producing.

The designer's role remains essential: guiding decisions, advocating for the user, exploring options, debating hierarchy and motion, and feeding that judgment back into the next build. The function of design does not change at its core. The drawn artifact just stops being the contract and becomes the conversation.

Where this goes next

This is a point of view, not a plan. The natural next step is a pilot: pick one surface, encode its design system as a contract in code, and let agents build from it end to end. The questions worth answering there are about strategy, trade-offs, and the migration path, and they deserve their own piece.
